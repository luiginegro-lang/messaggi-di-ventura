These phrases are drawn from the book of the same name, inspired by an idea born for the 2021 edition of The Feast of the Living (who reflect upon death), never realised due to the pandemic. The book was published by la casa cafausica in October 2022, with texts by Lu Cafausu — Giancarlo Norese, Luigi Negro, Luigi Presicce, Emilio Fantin, Cesare Pietroiusti — and made possible through the collaboration of Maria V. Tarantino, scholar of the tarot and tarologist. Buy the book
Some of these phrases are a decomposition of meanings related to the questions raised over time by the Lu Cafausu group on the subjects of living and dying; others emerge from a free reading of the tarot cards. With regard to the latter, both the major arcana and a selection of the minor arcana more closely connected to the themes celebrated in The Feast of the Living (who reflect upon death) have been taken into consideration.
The phrases contained in this publication may be freely used for exercises or experiments in divination; each person may decide their own method of use. Personalisation and individual care of the book are especially welcome: for instance, adding a dust jacket, pressing flowers or other plant forms between the pages and letting them dry, writing new phrases on added pages or on blank ones.
Lu Cafausu is the name chosen by a collective of artists, but also a real place: a small coffee house in Lecce stone, situated in San Cesario di Lecce. Located at the end of what was once the park of a villa from the early twentieth century — now completely vanished — it sits instead at the centre of a neighbourhood of apartment blocks and condominiums built from the late 1990s onward. Due to its particular conditions, Lu Cafausu has become metaphor and source of inspiration for stories, performances, rituals, pilgrimages, and actions in the town of San Cesario and in various European and American cities — symbol of something that is, at one and the same time, both central and marginal, where aesthetic contradictions meet the sense (or the senselessness) of our time. A chapel of desistance, of fragility, and at the same time of power. An imaginary place that truly exists.
The Feast of the Living (who reflect upon death) is a celebration coinciding with November 2nd, a collaborative project by Lu Cafausu that has for many years called upon artists from around the world to elaborate a reflection on death as transformation, threshold, mystery, but also as a necessary horizon of meaning. In various parts of the world, at the beginning of November, people celebrate the departed — by remembering those who are no longer here, by praying, by visiting places of burial. In one way or another, we all try to establish contact with our loved ones who are gone, and this often happens by considering our common destiny — that is, by reflecting on death in general. From the outset, Lu Cafausu has given life to an imaginary space where thoughts, analogies, and coincidences have sought to find echo in the voices and inner images aroused by the encounter with the dead, or by the awareness of the destiny of the living, through various modes of practice, reflection, or meditation that may create a space between life and death, between the departed and the mortals. The awareness of our common and ineluctable destiny can create forms of encounter and sensitivity among the participants in the feast, whoever they may be.
